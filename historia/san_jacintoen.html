SAN JACINTO BATTLE

The battle of San Jacinto on September 14, 1856, in which a group of Nicaraguan patriots clashed with a band of North American freebooters, is one of the most memorable events in the history of Nicaragua. Colonel José Dolores Estrada had received orders not to engage the enemy, except if his retreat was cut off. He had been sent to the plains of Tipitapa to block the activities of the filibusters at the head of enthusiastic young men poorly armed with flintlocks, machetes, and spears. They had traveled hundreds of kilometers through the thick mountains of Chontales and Matagalpa, climbing hills, crossing rivers, where they were looking for flint for their rifles.

They arrived at the San Jacinto hacienda at the end of August 1856. It happened then that a guerrilla found a gang of Yankees near Tipitapa, whom they ambushed and one of whom they took prisoner to the hacienda. For Colonel Estrada, this was a signal to fall back and avoid a direct confrontation with the filibusters, according to the instructions received. In an impromptu council they discussed what to do in the face of the imminent enemy attack. They all voted to stay and face the attack.

A fit of inspiration: In a fit of inspiration and wielding his sword, Colonel Estrada said: “We will not return from here, but I will not be the one to give part of the defeat. You are young and you will be ashamed if you do not do your duty.” Everyone applauded and shouted victory slogans. Then they went where they had the Yankee tied up outside the house and they immediately shot him without spending more than four shots. Then they began to make barricades around the house-hacienda, anticipating that the enemy would try to attack them by surprise, but they already had the order to shoot at point-blank range and they were convinced that death was preferable to retreating. William Walker knew that the presence of legitimist troops in the surroundings of Tipitapa, so he entrusted Lieutenant-Colonel Edmund H. McDonald to cross the Tipitapa River to attack the enemy based on the San Jacinto hacienda, calculated at about 120 soldiers. On September 5, some 40 filibusters attack the Nicaraguan forces that resist from the San Jacinto house-hacienda for more than two hours, where they protect themselves thanks to its thick adobe walls and stone corrals.

Impact among filibusters: In Granada, the news inflamed many filibusters who did not conceive that Nicaraguan soldiers could have repelled a North American contingent. So they organized a new force, and left for Tipitapa, to then arrive in San Jacinto at dawn on September 14. A heavy fog surrounds the hacienda and the force of 60 filibusters is organized into three companies, one under the command of Captain Lewis D. Watkins, another under the direction of Lieutenant Roberto Milligan and a third under the command of Major Calvin O'Neill.


he stone thrown by Andrés Castro: The filibusters attacked with their revolvers and rifles and wreaked havoc in their first attack, not without paying dearly for their attack. They crossed the corral and made the Nicaraguan forces fall back inside the house. The soldier, Andrés Castro, unable to use his rifle, used stones to shoot down and kill a filibuster. In the midst of the confusion and the permanent attack of the filibusters who had barricaded themselves behind the stone walls, Colonel Estrada sent a guerrilla from the left flank, to attack the enemy from the rear. The victory of the Nicaraguan patriots in San Jacinto over the filibuster forces would break the myth of their invincibility and would open a new juncture in the Central American patriotic war.
