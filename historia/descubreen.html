DISCOVERING OF NICARAGUA

Originally inhabited by the Nicaraos, Nicaragua was discovered in 1502 by Columbus during his fourth voyage. and, explored by Gil González Dávila (1522-1524) and by Hernández de Córdoba (1524), who conquered part of the territory and founded the cities of Granada, León and Segovia.

On the southeastern coast of Nicaragua, the "conchero" (cluster of shells) known as "Angie" is located, where the oldest known traces of human presence are found, dating back 8000 years.

Indigenous settlements in the region of the Nicaraguan Pacific Ocean coast are known to date back to 6000 BC. C. The site of Acahualinca (between 232 and 8 BC) is later, likewise other archaeological evidence is known, mainly ceramic articles and statuary of volcanic stone, such as those found on the island of Zapatera.

In the paleontological panorama of America, Nicaragua occupies one of the first places. This is indicated by more than twelve archaeological sites with petrified bones of prehistoric animals, distributed in the North, Center and South of the Country. Just to give two examples, let us cite the bison tracks detected in “El Recreo”, department of Managua; and the fossil deposit of "El Bosque" -twelve kilometers from Pueblo Nuevo, on the road between that town and San Juan de Limay- which in 1976, after a long scientific excavation, was the scene of a Symposium of paleontologists and archaeologists.

The first current was located in the tropical dry forest of the Pacific. Belonging to the linguistic families of North America (Hokan-Siux, Oto-Mangue and Uto-Aztec), it consisted of various peoples: Maribios or Sutiavas, Mangues or Chorotegas, and Nahuas or Nicaraguas (also called Niquiranos). Settled on plains near lakes and volcanoes, these cultures were organized in towns or agrarian villages under theocratic governments headed by menexics (councils of güegües or old men) or by teytes (or chiefs). Totally related to the superior towns of Mexico, they maintained an active barter or exchange trade with neighboring towns.
